About Me

First part of my life

I believe I have quite an interesting background especially when talking about where I’m from. I always have a tough time answer the question, “Where are you from?” The reason being, I was born in India where I spent maybe the first two years of my life, then moved and grew up in Thailand. I stayed there for a good 15 years, as my father got a job there. However, in spite of being there so long, I was not considered a citizen due to visa conflicts. I attended one school for 13 years, Ekamai International School. It was where I went for elementary, middle, and high school. The curriculum was american-based and so I had a good exposure to learning English and how the education system worked. Thirteen years in the same school gifted me with some life long friends whom I still keep in touch with, though I’m on the other side of the world now. It was absolutely amazing growing up in such a beautiful country filled with really humble people. I’m quite certain it’s because of how others were, that I’ve grown to be who I am today, somewhat humble. After having spent so much time in Bangkok, it was time for graduation and I had to make the decision of whether to stay and continue my education or to leave to another country. The decision was nerve-wracking! Leaving parents, not having a constant money supply, living on my own in a new country, and dealing with so many new things was a scary thought. Nonetheless, I’m glad I made the right decision and decided to pursue a mechanical engineering degree in the States.

COD Life

“Wow! This is just like the movies!” is how I reacted to everything I saw when I stepped onto US soil. I was situated in the Chicago Suburbs, Glen Ellyn, where I attended college at the College of DuPage. I will forever be grateful to how amazing COD is and will always recommended it to anyone looking to obtain education for a cheaper price than universities. The campus is B-E-A-utiful! I had classes with some of the best professors of the college and was extremely satisfied for the education I received. As an international student, I had to rent out an apartment close to the college before flying into the States. As it was the first time, it was obviously scary for a 17 year old to figure out how to deal with the apartment lease, but things worked out as they always do. I remember the first year here was maybe one of the most adventurous years of my life. I had to find ways to buy groceries without a car, study at home without a table, sleep on an air mattress (didn’t want to buy a bed), cook, and basically manage everything in my life. It wasn’t tough, but rather quite fun to have so many new things to deal with. There certainly were times where it felt lonely to be in a country so far from where my parents were, but I always found ways to deal with it. I learned how to handle my emotions like stress, sadness, loneliness, and happiness. I spent a good three years at COD, where I met some amazing people whom I wish I was still in contact with. The amazing folks welcomed and taught me a lot about how things worked here. Life was great, but time caught on and I had to continue moving forward. COD was not a 4-year institution and therefore didn’t hand out bachelor degrees but only associates. When I was done with my Associates in Mechanical Engineering at COD, I decided to transfer to Illinois Institute of Technology.

It’s been my second year at IIT and I’m a graduating senior this coming May (2020)! For the longest time, I was contemplating whether I should pursue a master’s degree but just before the deadline, I decided I was done studying and wanted to gain industry experience. Maybe if I took four years instead of five years to complete my bachelors, I would have gone ahead with getting a master’s degree, but that wasn’t the case so I decided otherwise. A Bachelor’s in Mechanical Engineering at IIT has been a different experience than what I went through at COD. Undoubtedly, a 4 year institution doesn’t have small classes like a community college typically does, the community is different, and so is the student - teacher connection. Nonetheless, I’ve enjoyed my experience here and am glad I had the chance to learn some pretty cool things in the Mechanical Engineering courses I’ve taken. So far, this last semester has been my favorite as I’ve signed up for courses that I’m very much interested in addition to the required courses needed to obtain my degree. I have a good load of 7 courses this semester and would have signed up for more if I could, but time is limited and priorities are important. One of my favorite classes I have really been looking forward to is COM 330 with Professor Stolley. I’ve always had a thing for coding in different languages, but haven’t quite got the grasp of web-development as much as others. When looking through the list of Humanities courses, I was certain I wanted to enroll in COM 330 as it was being offered and I don’t see who shouldn’t take the course. Nearly everyone loves talking about themselves and so why not make a portfolio of the amazing things you’ve done and post it online for others to learn from. Just a year ago, I had started building my own website in WordPress, filling it with some of my content and decorating it a bit. However, once classes started last semester I stopped working on it, but this semester I’ve been really happy to have a course dedicated to working on my site. By the end of this course, I hope to be able to design and maintain my own website on my very own domain.

Job Search

The hardest part of this semester is job searching while doing my best in the last batch of classes. Job searching as an international student is not easy at all. Unlike locals, we have the pressure of being kicked out of the country if employment is not guaranteed within a certain amount of time. Finding a company that’s willing to sponsor a work-visa and furthermore a green card in the future is a painstaking process, but there are ways to go about it. I’ve learned a whole lot about the process and am still in pursuit of a full-time position. I’ve hears stories of how people apply to 500+ companies and maybe get a reply from 1 or 2 - how depressing. I honestly, don’t have the time and can’t be bothered to write out 500 cover letters and resumes for each application and so I’ve been trying out a different approach. I’m certain that getting a referral holds a lot more weight than a resume in a stack of 300 other candidates. Therefore, I try to reach out to people in the field of my interest and build rapport. Building rapport can lead to a referral and from there, a position of your choosing. It’s a pity, but also a blessing that not everyone goes through the experience of living independently in a new country and figuring out life as it comes.
